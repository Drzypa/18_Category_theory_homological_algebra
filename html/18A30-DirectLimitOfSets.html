direct limit of sets


Let 𝒜={Ai∣i∈I} be a family of sets indexed by a non-empty set I. 𝒜 is said to be a direct family if

  1. 1.

    I is a directed set,

  2. 2.

    whenever i≤j in I, there is a function ϕi⁢j:Ai→Aj,

  3. 3.

    ϕi⁢i is the identity function on Ai,

  4. 4.

    if i≤j≤k, then ϕj⁢k∘ϕi⁢j=ϕi⁢k.

In the last condition, if we write a⁢ϕi⁢j:=ϕi⁢j⁢(a) for a∈Ai, then the equation can be rewritten as ϕi⁢j⁢ϕj⁢k=ϕi⁢k.

For example, the natural numbers ℕ={1,2,…,n,…} can be regarded as a direct family. Here, for any i≤j, ϕi⁢j:i→j is given by the natural injection ϕi⁢j⁢(ℓ):=ℓ for any ℓ∈i.

Let 𝒜 be a direct family of sets, indexed by I. Take the disjoint union of the members of 𝒜 and call it A (this can be achieved even when the members themselves have non-empty intersections, simply form the product Ai×{i} first before taking the union). Therefore, A has the properties that

  • •

    for any a∈A, a∈Ai for some i∈I, and

  • •

    if a∈Ai and b∈Aj and i≠j, then a≠b.

Define a binary relation ∼ on A as follows: given that a∈Ai and b∈Aj, a∼b iff there is Ak such that ϕi⁢k⁢(a)=ϕj⁢k⁢(b).

Proposition 1.

∼ on A is an equivalence relation.

Proof.

Clearly, ∼ is symmetric. By condition 2 of a direct family, ∼ is also reflexive. Now, suppose a∼b and b∼c with a∈Ai, b∈Aj and c∈Ak. So there are p,q∈I such that ϕi⁢p⁢(a)=ϕj⁢p⁢(b) and ϕj⁢q⁢(b)=ϕk⁢q⁢(c). Since I is directed, there is r∈I such that p,q≤r. From this, we have ϕi⁢r⁢(a)=ϕp⁢r⁢(ϕi⁢p⁢(a))=ϕp⁢r⁢(ϕj⁢p⁢(b))=ϕj⁢r⁢(b). Similarly, ϕk⁢r⁢(c)=ϕq⁢r⁢(ϕk⁢q⁢(c))=ϕq⁢r⁢(ϕj⁢q⁢(b)). Hence a∼c. ∎

Definition. Let 𝒜 be a direct family of sets indexed by I. Let A and ∼ be defined as above. Then the quotient A/∼ is called the direct limit of the sets in 𝒜. The direct limit of sets Ai is sometimes written A∞, or lim→⁡Ai. Elements of A∞ are sometimes denoted by [a]I or [a] whenever there is no confusion.

Remarks.

  • •

    This definition is consistent with the formal definition of direct limits in a category. The index I, being a directed set, can be viewed as a category whose objects are elements of I and morphisms defined by the partial order on I.

  • •

    The notation A∞ comes from the following fact: if |I|=n<∞, then lim→⁡Ai≅An. Here, ≅ stands for bijection.

  • •

    For every i∈I, there is a natural mapping Ai→A∞, given by a↦[a]I. This map may be variously denoted by ϕi, ϕi⁢∞, or ϕi⁢I.

  • •

    Let J be a subset of a directed set I. Let 𝒜 be a direct family indexed by I and 𝒜′⊆𝒜 indexed by J. Form the direct limit A∞′ of sets in 𝒜′. Then there is a natural mapping ϕJ⁢I:A∞′→A∞ such that for any j∈J, ϕJ⁢I∘ϕj⁢J=ϕj⁢I.

The dual notion of a direct limit of sets is that of an inverse limit. Instead of starting from a direct family of sets, we start with an inverse family of sets, which is defined similarly to that to of a direct family, except I is a filtered set, and the mappings ϕi⁢j:Ai→Aj is defined whenever j≤i. An inverse family is also known as an inverse system, or a projective system.
