limit of a functor


Let G be a functor from categories ℐ to 𝒞. A limit of G is a pair (L,τ) where

  1. 1.

    L:ℐ→𝒞 is a constant functor,

  2. 2.

    τ:L→G is a natural transformation,

such that it is universal among all pairs satisfying (1) and (2). In other words, for any pair (M,φ), where M:ℐ→𝒞 is a constant functor, and φ:M→G is a natural transformation, there is a unique natural transformation ϕ:M→L with the following commutative diagram:

\xymatrix@C+=64ptM\ar[dr]φ\ar@.>[dd]ϕ&GL\ar[ur]τ

Since we may identify a constant functor with its value, say an object A, in 𝒞, a limit of G may be viewed as an object A in 𝒞, together with a collection of morphisms A→G⁢(I), or GI, for each object I in ℐ such that

if ⁢I→J⁢ is a morphism, then ⁢A→GI→GJ=A→GJ.   ⁢(*)

Furthermore, if another object B in 𝒞 satisfies (*), then there is a unique morphism B→A such that

B→A→GI=B→GI

for all objects I in ℐ.

Remarks.

  • •

    A limit of a functor may or may not exist. If it exists, then it is unique up to natural equivalence. In other words, we may call it the limit of G.

  • •

    In the literature, the limit of G is variously known as the inverse limit, left limit, projective limit, root, or left root of G. and is generally written lim←⁡G.

  • •

    The category ℐ above is usually called the index category, and the functor G a diagram in 𝒞.

  • •

    The most common index categories are finite categories, discrete categories, partially ordered categories, and more specifically, directed categories.

  • •

    On the other hand, it turns out that if, in a category, if equalizers of any pairs of morphisms, and arbitrary products of any collections of objects exist, then it can be shown that every functor into this category has a limit.

By reversing all the arrows in 𝒞, we arrive at the dual concept of a limit, that of a colimit. Precisely, the colimit of G:ℐ→𝒞 is a pair (R,τ) where R:ℐ→𝒞 is a constant functor and τ:G→R is a natural transformation, such that for any pair (M,φ) of constant functor M:ℐ→𝒞 and natural transformation φ:G→M, there is a unique natural transformation ϕ:R→M such that the following diagram

\xymatrix@C+=64pt&R\ar@.>[dd]ϕG\ar[ur]τ\ar[dr]φ&M

The colimit is also known as the direct limit, right limit, inductive limit, coroot, or right root, and is written lim→.

Examples. Some common examples of limits (inverse limits) are products, terminal objects, pullbacks, equalizers, kernels, and kernel pairs. These examples can be readily verified. Let us verify that a terminal object is a limit:

Let G be the empty functor (from the empty category) into an arbitrary category 𝒞. So the limit of G is just an object C in 𝒞, and that’s it, as there are no objects in the empty category, there are no morphisms from C in the limit of G. If A is any object in 𝒞, then there is a unique morphism A→C, and that’s it. But this means that C is just a terminal object of 𝒞.
