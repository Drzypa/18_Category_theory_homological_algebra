attaching pullback squares


Pullback squares can be attached together to form larger pullback squares. The following proposition describes the main step involved in this attaching procedure.

Proposition.

Let a commutative diagram

\xymatrix⁢A⁢\ar⁢[r]⁢\ar⁢[d]⁢&⁢B⁢\ar⁢[r]⁢\ar⁢[d]⁢&⁢C⁢\ar⁢[d]⁢D⁢\ar⁢[r]⁢&⁢E⁢\ar⁢[r]⁢&⁢F

be given.

Assume that B is a pullback for E→F←C. Then the following are equivalent:

  • •

    A is a pullback for D→E←B.

  • •

    A is a pullback for D→F←C, where D→F is the composition D→E→F.

Proof.

First assume that A is a pullback for D→E←B. Let Z be the vertex of a limiting cone D←Z→C over D→F←C. We must find a unique filler for the diagram

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrrd]\ar@.>[rd]&&&&A\ar[r]\ar[d]&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&C\ar[d]&D\ar[r]&E\ar[r]&F

If we let Z→E denote the composition Z→D→E, then Z is the vertex of a limiting cone E←Z→C over E→F←C. Since B is a pullback for E→F←C, it follows that Z must factor uniquely through B, that is, there is a unique filler Z→B for the diagram

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrrd]\ar@.>[rrd]&&&&&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&C\ar[d]&D\ar[r]&E\ar[r]&F

But now observe that D←Z→B is a limiting cone over D→E←B. Since we assumed that A is a pullback for D→E←B, we find that Z must factor through A. The commutativity of the intermediate diagrams implies that this filler Z→A completes

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrrd]\ar[rrd]\ar@.>[rd]&&&&A\ar[r]\ar[d]&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&C\ar[d]&D\ar[r]&E\ar[r]&F

to a commutative diagram. Hence A is a pullback for D→F←C.

Now we must prove the converse. The reasoning is similar. Assume that A is a pullback for D→F←C. Suppose Z is the vertex of a limiting cone D←Z→B over D→E←B. By composing Z→B with B→C, we get that D←Z→C is a limiting cone over D→F←C. Applying the assumption that A is a pullback from D→F←C, it follows that there is a unique filler Z→A for the diagram

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrrd]\ar@.>[rd]&&&&A\ar[r]\ar[d]&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&C\ar[d]&D\ar[r]&E\ar[r]&F

Since Z→C is just the composition Z→B→C, we conclude that in fact Z→A is the unique filler for the diagram

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrd]\ar@.>[rd]&&&A\ar[r]\ar[d]&B\ar[d]&D\ar[r]&E

Hence A is a pullback for D→E←B. ∎

The left-to-right half of this proposition states that if two pullback squares are attached along a common edge, the outer rectangle of the resulting commutative diagram is also a pullback square. The right-to-left half says that if two commutative squares are attached so that the outer rectangle and the right square are both pullback squares, then the left square is a pullback square too. It is important to note what this does not say. One might ask whether we could assume instead that the outer rectangle and the left square are pullback squares and obtain that the right square is a pullback square. In this case we would need to find a filler for the diagram

\xymatrixZ\ar[rdd]\ar[rrd]\ar@.>[rd]&&&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&C\ar[d]&E\ar[r]&F

In order to apply the assumption that the outer rectangle is a pullback square, we would need to find a morphism Z→D, and to be able to exploit the commutativity of the diagram, this morphism must be compatible with Z→E above. These requirements together amount to demanding that there is a lifting

\xymatrix&D\ar[d]Z\ar[r]\ar@.>[ur]&E

of Z→E to a morphism Z→D. But not every morphism can be lifted, so this task is impossible in general.
