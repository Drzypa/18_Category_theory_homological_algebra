natural transformation


Definition. Let 𝒞 and 𝒟 be categories, and let S,T:𝒞→𝒟 be covariant functors. Then suppose that for every object A in 𝒞 one has a morphism ηA:S⁢(A)→T⁢(A) in 𝒟 such that for every morphism α:A→B in 𝒞 the following

\xymatrix⁢@+=4⁢p⁢c⁢S⁢(A)⁢\ar⁢[d]S⁢(α)⁢\ar⁢[r]ηA⁢&⁢T⁢(A)⁢\ar⁢[d]T⁢(α)⁢S⁢(B)⁢\ar⁢[r]ηB⁢&⁢T⁢(B)

is commutative (http://planetmath.org/CommutativeDiagram). Then we variously write

η:S⁢→˙⁢T  or  η:S⇒T  or  η:S→T

and call η a natural trasformation from S to T.

One may think of a natural transformation η:S→T as a ‘function’ from the class of objects of 𝒞 to the class of morphisms of 𝒟.

As a first example, for every functor S:𝒞→𝒟, we can associate the natural transformation 1S:S→S (the identity natural transformation on S) that assigns every object A of 𝒞, the corresponding identity morphism 1S⁢(A).

Natural transformations are composed in a similar manner to morphisms, but they are nevertheless defined as correspondences between both objects and morphisms as shown in the square commutative diagram depicted above.

More precisely, given three functors R,S,T:𝒞→𝒟, and two natural transformations, τ:R→S and η:S→T, we define the composition of τ with η, written η∙τ, as a class of morphisms in 𝒟 given by

(η∙τ)A:=ηA∘τA,

for every object A in 𝒞. It is easy to see that η∙τ is a natural transformation, since we may “compose” two commutative squares and obtain a third one:

\xymatrix@+=4pcR(A)\ar[d]R⁢(α)\ar[r]τA&S(A)\ar[d]S⁢(α)\ar[r]ηA&T(A)\ar[d]T⁢(α)\ar@[dr]|=&R(A)\ar[d]R⁢(α)\ar[r]ηA∘τA&T(A)\ar[d]T⁢(α)R(B)\ar[r]τB&S(B)\ar[r]ηB&T(B)&R(B)\ar[r]ηB∘τB&T(B)

It is easy to see that the composition “operation” on natural transformations is associative:

(ζ∙η)∙τ=ζ∙(η∙τ)

for natural transformations τ:R→S, η:S→T, and ζ:T→U. In addition, any identity natural transformation acts as a compositional identity: if τ:R→S and η:S→T, then

1S∙τ=τ   and   η∙1S=η.

Remarks.

  • •

    Natural transformations arise frequently in mathematics. Here’s a “concrete” example of a natural transformation: the boundary map Hn⁢(X,A)→Hn-1⁢(A) in a homology theory. By definition, every morphism of presheaves is a natural transformation. More prosaically, the determinant det:GLn⁡→˙⁢()* is natural.

  • •

    Natural transformations are sometimes called also functorial morphisms especially in applications related to the category theory development line pursued by Charles Ehresmann and the ‘Nicolas Bourbaki’ group; this is also a natural translation of the same concept from French, viz. (ref. [3]).
