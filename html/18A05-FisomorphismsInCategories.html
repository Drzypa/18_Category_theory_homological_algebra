F-isomorphisms in categories


Let 𝒞 and 𝒟 be categories, F:𝒞→𝒟 be a (covariant or contravariant) functor and let α∈Hom⁢(A,B) be a morphism, where A,B∈Ob⁢(𝒞).

Definition. A morphism α:A→B is an F-isomorphism if F⁢(α) is an isomorphism in 𝒟.

Note that each isomorphism in 𝒞 is an F-isomorphism for each functor F. The converse is true in the following sense: if α is an F-isomorphism for each functor F then α is an isomorphism. On the other hand there are F-isomorphisms which are not isomorphisms.

Also note, that if α:X→Y is an F-isomorphism, then there does not have to exist morphism β:Y→X which is ,,F-inverse” to α. Indeed, there may be no F-isomorphism from Y to X, see examples:

Example. 1) Let X be an object in 𝒟 and define FX:𝒞→𝒟 as follows: for A∈Ob⁢(𝒞) put FX⁢(A)=X and for α∈Hom⁢(A,B) put FX⁢(α)=idX. This is the constant functor and every morphism in 𝒞 is an FX-isomorphism (although it does not have to be an isomorphism). In particular, it may happen that for some objects X and Y in 𝒞 there is a morphism from X to Y but no morphism from Y to X. In this case there is an FX-isomorphism from X to Y but not vice versa.

2) Let 𝒯⁢op* be the category of pointed topological spaces and continous maps preserving based point, 𝒮⁢et be the category of sets and functions, 𝒢⁢r be the category of groups and homomorphisms. Consider the functor π:𝒯⁢op*→𝒮⁢et×𝒢⁢r×𝒢⁢r×⋯ defined by:

π⁢(X,x0)=(π0⁢(X,x0),π1⁢(X,x0),π2⁢(X,x0),π3⁢(X,x0),…);
π⁢(f)=(π0⁢(f),π1⁢(f),π2⁢(f),π3⁢(f),…),

where πn is the n-th homotopy group functor. Then π-isomorphism is a weak homotopy equivalence and it is known (due to Whitehead) that each weak homotopy equivalence between pointed CW-complexes is the homotopy equivalance. On the other hand there are weak homotopy equivalences which are not homotopy equivalences.

The concept of F-isomorphism is especially important in representation theory, where F is the homology functor from category of complexes over an abelian category 𝒞 to 𝒞.
