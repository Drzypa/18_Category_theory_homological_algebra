partial ordering on subobjects of an object


Let 𝒞 be a category and A an object 𝒞. Recall that a subobject of A is just an equivalent class of equivalent monomorphisms into A. A subobject is denoted by [f:B→A], where f is monomorphic, or simply [B], whenever there is no confusion. Furthermore, we write B⊆A to mean that B is a subobject of A, and write B≅C whenever [B]=[C]. The class of all subobjects of A is denoted by Sub⁢(A). We wish to put a partial order on Sub⁢(A).

Given any two monomorphisms f:B→A and g:C→A, define g≤f iff there is a morphism h:C→B such that

\xymatrix⁢C⁢\ar⁢[d⁢r]g⁢\ar⁢[d⁢d]h⁢&⁢A⁢B⁢\ar⁢[u⁢r]f

Now, if f′:B′→A is equivalent to f and g′:C→A is equivalent to g, then we have morphisms x:C→C′ and x′:C′→C and y:B→B′ and y′:B′→B such that the diagram below consisting of only the solid lines is commutative:

\xymatrixC\ar[dr]g\ar[dd]h\ar[rr]x&&C′\ar[dl]g′\ar[ll]x′\ar@.>[dd]h′&A&B\ar[ur]f\ar[rr]y&&B′\ar[ul]f′\ar[ll]y′

We define h′=y∘h∘x′ (the dotted line above). Then the diagram above including the dotted line is commutative: as f′∘h′=f′∘(y∘h∘x′)=f∘(h∘x′)=g∘x′=g′. Hence g′≤f′. As a result, ≤ induces a binary relation on Sub⁢(A):

C≤B

iff there are representing monomorphisms f:B→A and g:C→A such that g≤f. Let us use the same notation ≤ for the induced relation. Then ≤ on Sub⁢(A) is a partial ordering on Sub⁢(A):

reflexivity:

B≤B, because the composition of the identity morphism 1B with any representing monomorphism f:B→A is f itself.

anti-symmetry:

if B≤C and C≤B, then there are representing monomorphisms f:B→A and g:C→A such that f≤g and g≤f. So there are morphisms h:B→C and h′:C→B such that

\xymatrix⁢C⁢\ar⁢@⁢<0.5⁢e⁢x>⁢[d⁢r]g⁢\ar⁢@⁢<0.5⁢e⁢x>⁢[d⁢d]h′⁢&⁢A⁢B⁢\ar⁢@⁢<-0.5⁢e⁢x>⁢[u⁢r]f⁢\ar⁢@⁢<0.5⁢e⁢x>⁢[u⁢u]h

is commutative. But this just means that h and h′ are inverses of one other, or B≅C, so that they are the same subobject of A.

transitivity:

if D≤C and C≤B, then there are representing monomorphisms f:B→A, g:C→A and h:D→A such that h≤g and g≤f. This means there are morphisms r:D→C and s:C→B such that g∘r=h and f∘s=g. Since f∘(s∘r)=g∘r=h, h≤f, and as a result, D≤B.

Thus, ≤ turns Sub⁢(A) into a partially ordered class, with top element A. With this, we may form the notions of unions and intersections of subobjects. Formally, let {Bi∣i∈I} be a collection of subobjects of A, indexed by a set I.

  • •

    The union C of Bi is the supremum of the Bi’s with respect to ≤, provided that it exists. In notation, we write

    C=⋁i∈IBi.
  • •

    The intersection D of Bi is the infimum of the Bi’s with respect to ≤, provided that it exists. In notation, we write

    D=⋀i∈IBi.

For example, let f:B→A and g:C→A be subobjects of A. Then the pullback of f and g, if it exists, is the intersection of B and C.

Remark. It can be shown that in any Abelian category, Sub⁢(A) under ≤ is a lattice for any object A.
