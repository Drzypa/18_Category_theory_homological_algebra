properties of direct product


Let 𝒞 be a category. This entry lists some of the basic properties of categorical direct product:

Proposition 1.

(uniqueness of products) A product (C,{πi}i∈I) of objects {Ci}i∈I, if it exists, is unique up to isomorphism.

Before proving this, let us observe first that if f:C→C is a morphism such that

πi=πi∘f (1)

then f=1C necessarily, since the universal property of product C, f is the unique morphism such that (1) holds, but then π1=π1∘1C as well, and this forces f=1C.

Proof.

If (D,{gi}i∈I) is another product of {Ci}i∈I, we get two unique morphisms x:D→C and y:C→D such that πi=gi∘y and gi=πi∘x for all i∈I. So πi=(πi∘x)∘y=πi∘(x∘y). From the previous paragraph, we see that x∘y=1C. Similarly, gi=gi∘(y∘x), so that y∘x=1D. This shows that C is isomorphic to D. ∎

This justifies writing ∏i∈ICi (with πi) as the product of {Ci}i∈I. In case I has cardinality 2, we write C1×C2 as the product of C1 and C2. Also, when I=∅, we set the product as any terminal object T in 𝒞.

Proposition 2.

If I is the disjoint union of J and K, then

∏i∈ICi≅∏j∈JCj×∏k∈KCk,

assuming all products exist.

Proof.

Let

C=∏i∈ICi,D=∏j∈JCj,and E=∏k∈KCk.

We break down the proof into two cases:

  • •

    Suppose one of J and K is the empty set, say, J=∅. Then D is a terminal object, and K=I, so that E=C. In other words, we want to show that

    C≅D×C.

    First, notice that we have morphisms 1C:C→C and eC:C→D (where eC is unique since D is terminal). If A is any object with morphisms f:A→C and eA:A→D. Any g:A→C with f=1C∘g and eA=eC∘g must result in f=g. This shows that C may be viewed as the product of D and C, or C≅D×C.

  • •

    Now, suppose neither J nor K is empty. We have projection morphisms fi:C→Ci for all i∈I, gj:D→Cj for all j∈J, and hk:E→Ck for all k∈K. Write F=D×E with projections p1:F→D and p2:F→E.

    For every i∈I, define morphisms xi:F→Ci as follows: if i∈J, then xi=gi∘p1. Otherwise, xi=hi∘p2. Since J and K are disjoint, this I-indexed set of morphisms is well-defined. By the universality of the product C, we get a unique morphism x:F→C such that xi=fi∘x.

    Next, from the universal properties of the products D and E, we have two unique morphisms y:C→D and z:C→E such that fj=gj∘y and fk=hk∘z for any j∈J and k∈K. From the morphisms y:C→D and z:C→E and the universality of the product F, we have another unique morphism f:C→F such that y=p1∘f and z=p2∘f.

    Then p1∘(f∘x)=(p1∘f)∘x=y∘x. Since gj∘y∘x=fj∘x=xj=gj∘p1 for any j∈J, we have y∘x=p1, so that p1∘(f∘x)=p1. Similarly, p2∘(f∘x)=p2. This shows that f∘x=1F. Also, fi∘(x∘f)=(fi∘x)∘f=xi∘f. Now, if i∈J, then xi∘f=gi∘p1∘f=gi∘y=fi, and if i∈K, then xi∘f=hi∘p2∘f=hi∘z=fi. As a result, fi∘(x∘f)=fi for all i∈I, which implies x∘f=1C. This shows that C≅F=D×E.

This completes the proof. ∎

Corollary 1.

(commutativity of products) A×B≅B×A, if one (and hence the other) exists.

This shows that it does not matter whether we say A×B as the product of A and B, or the product of B and A.

Corollary 2.

(associativity of products) A×(B×C)≅A×B×C≅(A×B)×C, whenever the products are defined.

Remarks. All of the properties can be dualized, so that coproducts are unique up to isomorphism, and commutativity and associativity laws hold as well.
