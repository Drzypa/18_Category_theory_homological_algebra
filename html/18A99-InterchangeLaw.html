interchange law


Let S be a set and ∘ and ∙ two partial (http://planetmath.org/PartialFunction) binary operations on S. Then ∘ and ∙ are said to satisfy the interchange law if

(a∘b)∙(c∘d)=(a∙c)∘(b∙d),

provided that the operations are defined on both sides of the equation.

An element e∈S is a ∘-identity, or an identity with respect to ∘, if e∘a=a∘e=a provided the operations are defined.

Proposition 1.

If ∘ is a total function (defined for all of S×S), then there is at most one ∘-identity.

Proof.

If e and f are both ∘-idenities, then e=e∘f=f. ∎

Proposition 2.

If both ∘ and ∙ are total functions, and identities exist and the same with respect to both operations, then ∘⁣=⁣∙ and is commutative.

Proof.

Suppose that e is both the ∘-identity and the ∙-identity. Then, according to the interchange law, a∙d=(a∘e)∙(e∘d)=(a∙e)∘(e∙d)=a∘d, showing that ∙⁣=⁣∘. Again, using the interchange law, a∙d=(e∘a)∙(d∘e)=(e∙d)∘(a∙e)=d∘a=d∙a, showing that ∙ is commutative. ∎
